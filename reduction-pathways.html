Understanding Reduction Pathways

Is It a Calculator, a Theory, or Both?

🧠 The Answer: Both — and More

Reduction Pathways are not merely a carbon calculator, nor are they only a theoretical climate concept. They are a structured decarbonization approach that sits at the intersection of climate science and operational strategy — combining rigorous methodologies with data-driven modeling.

📐

The Scientific & Theoretical Layer

Reduction Pathways are grounded in climate science — including IPCC emissions scenarios, the GHG Protocol, and the Science Based Targets initiative (SBTi). These frameworks define what "good" looks like: how fast emissions must decline, which sectors need to transform, and what constitutes a 1.5°C-aligned trajectory. The theory provides the destination and the guardrails.

⚙️

The Modeling & Calculation Layer

Organizations translate theory into action through quantitative scenario modeling. Using historical emissions data, activity forecasts, and reduction levers (efficiency upgrades, electrification, renewable energy, offsets), companies build mathematical models that project future emissions under different assumptions. This is where the calculator meets the strategy — producing actionable, comparable pathways.

In practice: An organization uses reduction pathways to simulate "What happens if we electrify our fleet by 2028?" versus "What if we wait until 2032?" — comparing the emissions impact, cost, feasibility, and timeline of each choice. It is both a strategic planning framework and a quantitative modeling tool.

Key Features of Reduction Pathways

Designing Multiple Reduction Scenarios

Scenario A

Business-As-Usual

Projects future emissions based on current operations with no additional reduction measures. Serves as the baseline reference against which all other scenarios are measured. Essential for understanding the "do nothing" trajectory and its associated risks.

Scenario B

Ambitious Climate Action

Assumes accelerated deployment of all available reduction levers — full electrification, 100% renewable energy, supply chain engagement, and early adoption of emerging technologies. Aligns with a 1.5°C science-based target.

Scenario C

Cost-Optimized Pathway

Balances emissions reduction with financial constraints. Prioritizes abatement measures with the lowest cost per ton of CO₂ (marginal abatement cost curve). Ideal for organizations with capital limitations who need the most impact per dollar invested.

Scenario D

Delayed Transition

Models a slower start to decarbonization with aggressive late-stage reductions. Illustrates the risk of delayed action — higher cumulative emissions, greater reliance on negative emissions technologies, and increased transition risk exposure.

Our Approach

Recommendations for Effective Reduction Pathways

  • 1
    Use Verified and Consistent Sustainability Data

    Pathway models are only as reliable as the data feeding them. Ensure emissions data is verified, consistent across reporting periods, and covers all material sources. Avoid estimation gaps that undermine scenario credibility.

  • 2
    Clearly Define Organizational and Operational Boundaries

    Establish which entities, facilities, and activities are included. Use the GHG Protocol's operational and organizational boundary frameworks to ensure consistency between your baseline and all projected scenarios.

  • 3
    Include Scope 1, Scope 2, and Scope 3 Emissions

    A credible reduction pathway addresses all three scopes. Scope 3 often represents the majority of an organization's carbon footprint and cannot be excluded from long-term decarbonization planning.

  • 4
    Update Reduction Models Annually

    Pathways are living tools, not static documents. Revisit and recalibrate annually to reflect new data, technology developments, regulatory changes, and organizational shifts. An outdated pathway can lead to misinformed decisions.

  • 5
    Align Outputs with Reporting Frameworks

    Ensure your reduction pathway outputs map directly to disclosure and reporting requirements, including CDP, CSRD (European Sustainability Reporting Standards), GRI, and TCFD recommendations. This reduces duplication of effort and ensures regulatory readiness.

  • 6
    Continuously Monitor Performance Against Milestones

    Establish quarterly or biannual review cycles to track actual emissions against pathway projections. Early identification of deviations allows for corrective action before targets are missed. Use live dashboards for real-time visibility.
